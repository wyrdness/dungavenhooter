## Overview

The Dungavenhooter is a 'fearsome critter' from the lumber camps of the northeastern and Great Lakes United States. It is described as a large crocodylian beast with no mouth, instead possessing huge nostrils. From cover among brush, the Dungavenhooter ambushes lumberjacks — preferring those who have been drinking — and beats them into a vapour with its powerful tail before inhaling the resulting mist through its nose. The creature is recorded in Henry H. Tryon's 'Fearsome Critters' (1939), part of the small canon of published lumberwoods folklore that includes works by William T. Cox and Art Child.

## Theories & Explanations

### Skeptical

**Occupational tall tale** _(Henry H. Tryon, William T. Cox, Folklorists of American occupational lore)_

The Dungavenhooter is a deliberate lumber-camp invention — a 'greenhorn-scarer' yarn told to new recruits — and is best understood as folklore, not zoology.
